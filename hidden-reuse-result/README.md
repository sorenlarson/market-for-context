# Hidden Reuse, Firm Size, Platform Boundaries, and Value Capture

> An AI provider can learn after the sale. When that learning cannot be
> credibly deterred or priced, a context owner responds by sharing less
> information or moving the activity inside the firm.

This repository contains four linked theoretical and computational models: a
bilateral hidden-reuse game, a free-formation extension for equilibrium firm
size, and a heterogeneous network model in which customer access can substitute
for ownership, plus a value-appropriation extension asking whether an AI
intermediary can charge for the operating value its learning creates. It is the
canonical self-contained package; no prior essay or conversation is required.

**Status:** calibrated theoretical computation, version 0.7.0. The figures are
model results, not empirical evidence about current AI markets.

**Start here:** [The Market for Context](FULL-EXPOSITION.md) is the single,
self-contained exposition of the bilateral contract, firm-size, platform
boundary, heterogeneous-network, and value-capture results.

## The result in one minute

A context owner needs an external AI provider to act on valuable private
information. Disclosure improves the current service, but the provider can
secretly reuse what it sees. Reuse can improve the provider's future capability,
let it demand better terms later, and erode the value the owner receives from
keeping its context exclusive.

Let \(G(s)\) be the provider's discounted private gain from reusing disclosure
\(s\). Monitoring intensity \(k\) exposes the provider to an expected sanction
\(Eks\). A non-reuse promise changes behavior exactly when the provider prefers
to comply rather than reuse:

\[
G(s)\le Eks.
\]

At full monitoring, deterrence is feasible if and only if
\(E\ge G(s)/s\). If deterrence fails, full disclosure can still survive when
the provider can compensate the owner for permission to retain and benefit from
what it learns. Otherwise the outcome moves toward withholding or ownership.

The pricing extension makes that compensation limit explicit. Let \(\mu\) be the
provider's expected net value from the future capability, \(W\) the collateral
or payment it can commit before the value is known, and \(\phi\) the share of
future value a contract can verify and collect. The most the provider can
credibly promise is

\[
P^*=\min\{\mu,\;W+\phi\mu\}.
\]

The first term is the provider's willingness to participate. The second is what
the owner can actually collect. Competition can push a price toward \(P^*\);
it cannot make the unobservable remainder enforceable. The pricing cap builds
on Holmström and Tirole's pledgeable-income logic, and the withholding regime
builds on disclosure-expropriation models (Anton and Yao; Baccara); see
[REFERENCES.md](REFERENCES.md).

If the provider privately learns whether its expected value is low or high
before contracting, the owner faces a second limit. It can post the low type's
financeable fee and serve both types, or post the high fee and risk serving only
the high type. Even successful screening leaves the high provider all value
above its pledgeability ceiling.

Here **modularity** means the owner and provider remain independent firms.
**Secure modularity** means they use all the context without provider reuse.

| Enforcement | Is future value pledgeable? | Integration cost | Governance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strong enough | Not required | Any compatible level | Secure modularity |
| Weak | Yes | Integration relatively costly | Priced reuse |
| Weak | No | High | Strategic withholding |
| Weak | No | Low | Ownership |

The table states the mechanism, not unconditional global thresholds. Total
contract value and each party's **outside option**—what it can earn if no
contract is signed—also determine which arrangement is chosen.

![Three-panel phase map of hidden-reuse governance regimes](outputs/hidden-reuse-regime-map.svg)

The dotted curve is the full-disclosure deterrence **feasibility** boundary.
Crossing it does not mechanically select secure modularity because monitoring
can remain too costly.

![Two-panel map of pledgeable capability value and governance](outputs/capability-pledgeability-map.svg)

The second figure replaces the assumed payment cap with collateral and
verifiable future value. Under the displayed weak-enforcement scenario, the
diagonal move from low to high pledgeability changes the result from
withholding to priced reuse.

Version 0.3 adds the firm-size result. If one integrated firm owns \(n\)
homogeneous context-generating assets, incremental surplus per asset is

\[
g(n)=A-\frac Kn
+L\frac{n-1}{\kappa+n-1}
-dn^{\rho-1}
-cn^\eta.
\]

Here \(A\) is the per-asset internalization advantage generated by the
bilateral hidden-reuse problem. Because it adds the same value at every size,
it determines whether integrated firms form but drops out of the conditional
size choice. Shared fixed costs \(K\), transferable learning \(L\), and
declining marginal integration-execution cost \(dn^\rho\) push conditional
size up; increasing ongoing coordination cost pushes it down.

![Firm-boundary and firm-size separation map](outputs/firm-size-separation-map.svg)

This yields the package's second central result: a reason to own an asset is not
yet a theory of concentration. Hidden reuse can move activity across the firm
boundary without explaining whether the resulting firm should own two assets
or twenty.

Version 0.4 adds the missing platform alternative. Let \(q\) be the fraction of
directed cross-node learning that an AI intermediary can realize while operating
assets remain independent customers. If it acquires a subset \(S\), the extra
learning obtained from ownership is only

\[
(1-q)\sum_{i,j\in S;\ i\ne j}\gamma_{ij}.
\]

Customer access and ownership are therefore substitute ways to aggregate
learning. Partial ownership can also endanger buyer-supplier relationships that
cross the new firm boundary. Strong customer conflict removes partial
structures and produces an all-or-nothing switch: low external learning
efficiency supports a full rollup; high external learning efficiency supports a
neutral platform.

![Platform, partial-rollup, and full-rollup phase map](outputs/ownership-access-regime-map.svg)

With heterogeneous assets, total learning is not enough to predict the
boundary. A second exact computation holds total learning fixed and changes only
which node types learn from which others; the equilibrium changes from a
platform to a specialized clinic rollup.

Version 0.5 separates value creation from value capture. Let $p$ be the share
of AI-created operating value an intermediary captures through service prices
and $o$ the share it retains under ownership after acquisition-price
capitalization and downstream pass-through. For an ownership subset $S$, the
new private-value terms are

\[
(o-p)\left[B(S)+q\bigl(\Gamma_I(S)+\Gamma_X(S)\bigr)\right]
+o(1-q)\Gamma_I(S).
\]

The first term is value already producible through customer access but more
readily retained at owned operations. The second is learning that ownership
actually makes possible. Ownership can therefore win even at $q=1$, when it
creates no additional learning. For partial ownership, learning imported from
outside customers can also make customer access and ownership complements.

![Access and value appropriation phase map](outputs/value-appropriation-regime-map.svg)

Version 0.6 tests the results against their own assumptions. Both capture
shares are now derived from one pledgeability friction applied to opposite
sides of two markets, so the owner share exceeds the platform share exactly
when less than half of AI-created value is pledgeable. The firm-size
separation is restated as a characterization—conditional size is invariant to
the internalization advantage if and only if that advantage is
size-independent—with a worked diluted-advantage example in which entry and
size interact. And the platform/full-rollup polarization is stress-tested:
it survives opening the network to never-acquirable fringe customers, but
under the firm-size chapter's own saturating learning form high customer
conflict compresses rather than eliminates partial ownership. The positioning
against prior literature is rewritten throughout; see
[REFERENCES.md](REFERENCES.md).

Version 0.7 separates integration execution from ongoing coordination in the
firm-size model. Cumulative execution cost \(dn^\rho\), with \(0<\rho<1\),
has declining marginal cost as an acquirer reuses its playbook, systems,
diligence, and financing infrastructure. Ongoing coordination cost
\(cn^{1+\eta}\) still rises convexly and supplies the eventual stopping force.
The revised calibration selects an eight-asset conditional target and Figure 3
now varies ongoing coordination cost while holding the integration learning
curve fixed.

## Reproduce everything

Python 3.9 or newer is required. From a clean checkout, one command creates an
isolated environment, installs the locked dependencies, and runs the full
audit:

```bash
make bootstrap
```

`make audit` runs the tests, regenerates the PNG, SVG, CSV, JSON, and worked
examples, compiles the Python source, and checks the cold-reader package for
missing files and broken local links.

For an already configured environment:

```bash
python3 -m pip install --upgrade pip
python3 -m pip install -e '.[dev]'
make reproduce
```

Every equilibrium solver is deterministic. The third and fourth results
additionally run fixed-seed synthetic perturbation exercises around stated
anchor scenarios; they are robustness evidence for the calibrations, not
empirical uncertainty.

## Claim discipline

The package establishes:

- the incentive-compatibility threshold for deterring hidden reuse;
- the maximum pledgeable-payment formula under uncertain, partly verifiable
  future capability value;
- the pooling-versus-screening boundary in a two-type private-signal
  posted-price benchmark;
- the transfer constraint that determines whether reuse can be priced;
- equilibrium governance choices in the stated baseline calibration;
- a core-stable target firm size in a homogeneous free-formation replica
  economy;
- the separation between integration entry and conditional firm size; and
- the external-learning-efficiency boundary between a neutral platform and a
  full rollup in the symmetric ownership-access model;
- the exact customer-conflict threshold that suppresses partial ownership;
- heterogeneous acquisition choices in a stated single-intermediary directed
  network, with exhaustive subset-deviation checks; and
- a constructive counterexample showing that equal aggregate learning can
  support different firm boundaries when learning topology changes;
- an exact decomposition of ownership value into capture and productive-learning
  upgrades;
- a symmetric pledgeability derivation of both capture shares, under which the
  owner share exceeds the platform share exactly when the pledgeable share of
  AI-created value is below one half;
- the platform/full-rollup capture boundary and the condition under which
  ownership remains privately preferred at complete external learning;
- a constructive counterexample in which better customer access induces
  selective ownership because outside learning is monetized at an owned target;
- comparative-static predictions that can later be taken to data.

It does **not** establish:

- that real AI providers currently reuse customer context;
- empirical values for enforcement, context loss, or integration cost;
- equilibrium concentration across the entire application/model/infrastructure
  stack, competition among multiple potential acquirers, or endogenous customer
  prices and exit; or
- endogenous acquisition bargaining or a general optimal pricing mechanism for
  operating value; or
- welfare optimality of ownership.

The binary reuse choice, predefined no-contract fallbacks, and the third
model's single-intermediary acquisition benchmark are deliberate assumptions.
See
[RESULT.md](RESULT.md#limitations-and-next-tests) and
[FIRM-SIZE-RESULT.md](FIRM-SIZE-RESULT.md#9-limitations-and-next-extensions),
plus
[OWNERSHIP-ACCESS-RESULT.md](OWNERSHIP-ACCESS-RESULT.md#10-limits-and-next-tests)
and [APPROPRIATION-RESULT.md](APPROPRIATION-RESULT.md#11-limits).
