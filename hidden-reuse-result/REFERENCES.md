# References and intellectual lineage

These references motivate the mechanism and solution concept. They are not
empirical validation of the calibration.

## Dispersed knowledge and information disclosure

- Hayek, Friedrich A. 1945. “The Use of Knowledge in Society.” *American
  Economic Review* 35(4): 519–530.
  [Primary text hosted by the American Economic Association](https://www.aeaweb.org/aer/top20/35.4.519-530.pdf).

  Hayek motivates the importance of context tied to particular circumstances
  of time and place.

- Arrow, Kenneth J. 1962. “Economic Welfare and the Allocation of Resources
  for Invention.” In *The Rate and Direction of Inventive Activity*.
  [Primary chapter hosted by the National Bureau of Economic Research](https://www.nber.org/books-and-chapters/rate-and-direction-inventive-activity-economic-and-social-factors/economic-welfare-and-allocation-resources-invention).

  Arrow motivates the appropriation problem created when valuable information
  must be revealed in order to be evaluated or used.

- Anton, James J., and Dennis A. Yao. 1994. “Expropriation and Inventions:
  Appropriable Rents in the Absence of Property Rights.” *American Economic
  Review* 84(1): 190–209.
  [JSTOR record](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2117977).

  Anton and Yao analyze strategic information disclosure when formal property
  rights are incomplete.

- Anton, James J., and Dennis A. Yao. 2002. “The Sale of Ideas: Strategic
  Disclosure, Property Rights, and Contracting.” *Review of Economic Studies*
  69(3): 513–531.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-937X.t01-1-00020).

  Anton and Yao show that partial disclosure is the equilibrium way to sell an
  expropriable idea, which makes their model the direct precursor of the
  strategic-withholding regime here. Their signaling channel—partial disclosure
  signals the value of what is withheld—is absent from the present model, which
  instead derives withholding from the enforcement and pledgeability margins.

## Pledgeable income and the financing of learning rights

- Aghion, Philippe, and Jean Tirole. 1994. “The Management of Innovation.”
  *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 109(4): 1185–1209.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.2307/2118360).

  Aghion and Tirole study how cash constraints on the research unit can block
  the efficient allocation of property rights over an innovation. The
  pledgeability limit on learning rights here plays the same role: the provider
  cannot always pay for the rights it values most.

- Holmström, Bengt, and Jean Tirole. 1997. “Financial Intermediation, Loanable
  Funds, and the Real Sector.” *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 112(3):
  663–691.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/003355397555316).

  Holmström and Tirole introduce pledgeable income: the part of a project's
  return that can credibly be promised to outsiders. The payment ceiling
  P\* = min{μ, W + φμ} in this package is an application of their
  pledgeable-income cap to the value of a learning right, and the comparative
  static ∂P\*/∂μ = φ when enforceability binds is the standard pledgeability
  observation.

- Holmström, Bengt, and Jean Tirole. 1998. “Private and Public Supply of
  Liquidity.” *Journal of Political Economy* 106(1): 1–40.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/250001).

  Holmström and Tirole show that when only part of future value is pledgeable,
  privately supplied claims can be insufficient in aggregate. The wedge between
  total and pledgeable value in their analysis is the same wedge that leaves
  part of the provider's learning value uncontractible here.

## Firm boundaries and incomplete contracting

- Coase, Ronald H. 1937. “The Nature of the Firm.” *Economica* 4(16): 386–405.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x).

  Coase supplies the basic market-versus-firm question.

- Grossman, Sanford J., and Oliver D. Hart. 1986. “The Costs and Benefits of
  Ownership: A Theory of Vertical and Lateral Integration.” *Journal of
  Political Economy* 94(4): 691–719.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/261404).

  Grossman and Hart motivate ownership as an allocation of residual control
  rights when contracts are incomplete.

- Hart, Oliver, and John Moore. 1990. “Property Rights and the Nature of the
  Firm.” *Journal of Political Economy* 98(6): 1119–1158.
  [Primary record hosted by Harvard](https://dash.harvard.edu/entities/publication/73120378-84b8-6bd4-e053-0100007fdf3b).

  Hart and Moore identify a firm with the nonhuman assets under common control
  and study how ownership affects investment incentives.

- Holmström, Bengt, and John Roberts. 1998. “The Boundaries of the Firm
  Revisited.” *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 12(4): 73–94.
  [Primary article hosted by the American Economic Association](https://pubs.aeaweb.org/doi/10.1257/jep.12.4.73).

  Holmström and Roberts emphasize that firm boundaries reflect a wider set of
  coordination and incentive instruments than asset ownership alone.

- Rajan, Raghuram G., and Luigi Zingales. 1998. “Power in a Theory of the
  Firm.” *Quarterly Journal of Economics* 113(2): 387–432.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1162/003355398555630).

  Rajan and Zingales propose regulated access to a critical resource as an
  alternative to ownership for generating power and incentives. That idea is
  the precursor of the ownership-access extension, which asks when customer
  access to operating context substitutes for owning the operation in an AI
  learning network.

- Baccara, Mariagiovanna. 2007. “Outsourcing, Information Leakage, and
  Consulting Firms.” *RAND Journal of Economics* 38(1): 269–289.
  [JSTOR record](https://www.jstor.org/stable/25046303).

  Baccara models an outside contractor that can secretly leak the information
  it acquires while performing a task and shows how that leakage risk drives
  the in-house versus outsourcing choice. This is the closest published
  precursor of the bilateral hidden-reuse result; the present model adds the
  enforcement margin and the pledgeability of the learning right, which
  together select among four governance regimes rather than a binary
  make-or-buy.

## Firm size, knowledge capital, and coalition formation

- Farrell, Joseph, and Suzanne Scotchmer. 1988. “Partnerships.” *Quarterly
  Journal of Economics* 103(2): 279–297.
  [Primary journal record](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/103/2/279/1827018).

  Farrell and Scotchmer study freely forming coalitions whose output is shared
  among members and establish stable partnership partitions. The homogeneous
  transferable-utility benchmark in this package uses the related logic that a
  coalition must maximize surplus per member to resist blocking.

- Garicano, Luis. 2000. “Hierarchies and the Organization of Knowledge in
  Production.” *Journal of Political Economy* 108(5): 874–904.
  [Primary journal record](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/317671).

  Garicano explains how the acquisition and communication of knowledge shape
  organizational form and hierarchy.

- Garicano, Luis, and Thomas N. Hubbard. 2003. “Specialization, Firms, and
  Markets: The Division of Labor Within and Between Law Firms.” NBER Working
  Paper 9719; later published in the *Journal of Law, Economics, and
  Organization*.
  [Primary working-paper page](https://www.nber.org/papers/w9719).

  Their evidence links firm boundaries to the value of knowledge sharing and
  the costs of monitoring, providing a useful empirical analogue for testing
  the separate boundary and scale margins here.

- Azoulay, Pierre. 2003. “Acquiring Knowledge Within and Across Firm
  Boundaries: Evidence from Clinical Development.” NBER Working Paper 10083;
  later published in the *American Economic Review*.
  [Primary working-paper page](https://www.nber.org/papers/w10083).

  Azoulay finds that knowledge-intensive clinical projects are more likely to
  be organized internally while data-intensive projects are more often
  outsourced, illustrating that knowledge and raw data need not cross firm
  boundaries in the same way.

- Markusen, James R. 1995. “The Boundaries of Multinational Enterprises and the
  Theory of International Trade.” *Journal of Economic Perspectives* 9(2):
  169–189.
  [Primary article hosted by the American Economic Association](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Fjep.9.2.169).

  Markusen treats knowledge capital as a mobile, partly joint input across
  geographically separated facilities. That is the closest established
  analogue to the sequel's shared AI fixed cost and cross-node learning terms.

- Korinek, Anton, and Jai Vipra. 2024. “Concentrating Intelligence: Scaling
  Laws and Market Structure in Artificial Intelligence.” NBER Working Paper
  33139.
  [Primary working-paper page](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33139).

  Korinek and Vipra analyze how scale and scope economies in foundation models
  can produce concentration, tipping, and vertical integration. The present
  result instead isolates firm size at the context-generating asset layer.

- Kumar, Krishna B., Raghuram G. Rajan, and Luigi Zingales. 1999. “What
  Determines Firm Size?” NBER Working Paper 7208.
  [Primary working-paper page](https://www.nber.org/papers/w7208).

  Kumar, Rajan, and Zingales distinguish technological and organizational
  determinants of firm size and document roles for capital intensity, R&D,
  finance, and judicial institutions. Those are natural sources of empirical
  moments for the scale parameters in this package.

The proposed contribution here is not the generic idea that fixed costs,
knowledge, integration experience, or organization costs determine firm size.
It is the explicit separation obtained after connecting those forces to the
hidden-reuse microfoundation: an additive Arrow–Coase internalization
advantage changes whether firms form, while shared cost, transferable
learning, declining marginal integration cost, and increasing ongoing
coordination cost determine how large they are conditional on forming.

## Platforms, customer access, and multilateral boundaries

- Hagiu, Andrei, and Julian Wright. 2015. “Multi-Sided Platforms.” Harvard
  Business School Working Paper 15-037.
  [Primary working paper](https://www.hbs.edu/ris/Publication%20Files/15-037_cb5afe51-6150-4be9-ace2-39c6a8ace6d4.pdf).

  Hagiu and Wright compare platforms with vertical integration as alternative
  allocations of control. Their coordination-versus-local-incentive tradeoff is
  the closest platform-theory precursor to the ownership-access comparison.

- Hagiu, Andrei, and Julian Wright. 2023. “Data-Enabled Learning, Network
  Effects, and Competitive Advantage.” *RAND Journal of Economics* 54(4):
  638–667.
  [Primary journal record](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/1756-2171.12453).

  This paper distinguishes learning across customers from learning through
  repeated use by one customer. The ownership-access extension asks whether
  across-customer learning requires common ownership.

- Gawer, Annabelle. 2021. “Digital Platforms' Boundaries: The Interplay of Firm
  Scope, Platform Sides, and Digital Interfaces.” *Long Range Planning* 54(5):
  102045.
  [Author manuscript and record](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3702057).

  Gawer separates the scope of assets owned, the customers admitted to a
  platform, and the interfaces through which data move. The present model makes
  those boundary choices interact through external learning efficiency and
  customer loss.

- Bolton, Patrick, and Michael D. Whinston. 1993. “Incomplete Contracts,
  Vertical Integration, and Supply Assurance.” *Review of Economic Studies*
  60(1): 121–148.
  [Primary journal record](https://academic.oup.com/restud/article-abstract/60/1/121/1575987).

  Bolton and Whinston show that multilateral production and distribution
  relations are necessary for explaining integration scope. Their insight
  motivates representing customer links as a network rather than repeating a
  bilateral make-or-buy decision.

- Hart, Oliver, and Jean Tirole. 1990. “Vertical Integration and Market
  Foreclosure.” *Brookings Papers on Economic Activity: Microeconomics*:
  205–286.
  [Primary working-paper page and text](https://ceepr.mit.edu/workingpaper/vertical-integration-and-market-foreclosure-2/).

  Hart and Tirole analyze how integration changes trade with unintegrated
  firms. The ownership-access model uses a reduced-form neutrality/customer
  loss rather than reproducing their price-and-foreclosure game.

- Lee, Robin S. 2013. “Vertical Integration and Exclusivity in Platform and
  Two-Sided Markets.” *American Economic Review* 103(7): 2960–3000.
  [Primary article and replication materials](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Faer.103.7.2960).

  Lee provides structural empirical evidence that ownership and compatibility
  can reshape participation and welfare in a two-sided platform. This is a
  natural empirical template for a future calibrated version of the present
  theoretical boundary result.

- Jackson, Matthew O., and Asher Wolinsky. 1996. “A Strategic Model of Social
  and Economic Networks.” *Journal of Economic Theory* 71(1): 44–74.
  [Author publication page](https://web.stanford.edu/~jacksonm/publishedsubject.html).

  Jackson and Wolinsky distinguish stable networks formed by private incentives
  from efficient networks. The current solver is narrower: it tests every
  ownership-subset deviation for one intermediary rather than claiming
  pairwise stability in a decentralized network-formation game.

The ownership-access contribution is not the generic claim that platforms and
vertical integration differ. It identifies an exact substitution margin—the
share of directed learning unavailable through independent customer access—and
an exact customer-conflict threshold above which partial ownership is dominated
by the neutral-platform and full-rollup endpoints. Its heterogeneous
counterexample also shows why total learning weight is not a sufficient
statistic for firm scope.

## Value creation and value appropriation

- Brandenburger, Adam M., and Harborne W. Stuart Jr. 1996. “Value-Based
  Business Strategy.” *Journal of Economics & Management Strategy* 5(1): 5–24.
  [Primary journal record](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1430-9134.1996.00005.x).

  Brandenburger and Stuart distinguish value created in a supplier-firm-buyer
  system from the share any one participant can appropriate. The present
  extension makes that distinction explicit inside a directed AI learning
  network.

- Teece, David J. 1986. “Profiting from Technological Innovation: Implications
  for Integration, Collaboration, Licensing and Public Policy.” *Research
  Policy* 15(6): 285–305.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1016/0048-7333(86)90027-2).

  Teece explains why innovators may fail to capture the value they create and
  why returns can accrue to owners of complementary assets. AI-enabled operating
  assets play the complementary-asset role in Result IV, while customer context
  also feeds the underlying learning process.

- Maskin, Eric, and Jean Tirole. 1999. “Unforeseen Contingencies and Incomplete
  Contracts.” *Review of Economic Studies* 66(1): 83–114.
  [Author-hosted primary text](https://maskin.scholars.harvard.edu/publications/unforeseen-contingencies-and-incomplete-contracts).

  Maskin and Tirole show that unforeseeability by itself need not prevent
  optimal contracting when future payoffs can be forecast and commitment is
  sufficiently rich. This is an important discipline on the appropriation
  result: the model requires verifiability, commitment, bargaining, or pricing
  constraints, not merely an assertion that future outcomes are complicated.

The value-appropriation contribution is not the generic statement that firms
can create more value than they capture. It supplies an exact decomposition
between value already producible through customer access and learning newly
created by ownership, then shows that outside-customer learning can complement
selective ownership when value lands at owned operations. That complementarity
is absent from the earlier learning-only model.

## Data markets and information reuse

- Bergemann, Dirk, and Alessandro Bonatti. 2019. “Markets for Information: An
  Introduction.” *Annual Review of Economics* 11: 85–107.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-economics-080315-015439).

  Bergemann and Bonatti survey markets in which information is directly priced
  and sold. The priced-reuse regime here is a market for a learning right
  rather than for an information good, but their framework is the natural home
  for the pricing questions this package treats in reduced form.

- Jones, Charles I., and Christopher Tonetti. 2020. “Nonrivalry and the
  Economics of Data.” *American Economic Review* 110(9): 2819–2858.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.20191330).

  Jones and Tonetti show that firms may inefficiently hoard nonrival data out
  of concern that its use elsewhere erodes their position. Their hoarding
  result is the macroeconomic twin of strategic withholding here, derived from
  a growth model rather than a bilateral contracting game.

- Acemoglu, Daron, Ali Makhdoumi, Azarakhsh Malekian, and Asu Ozdaglar. 2022.
  “Too Much Data: Prices and Inefficiencies in Data Markets.” *American
  Economic Journal: Microeconomics* 14(4): 218–256.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1257/mic.20200200).

  Acemoglu, Makhdoumi, Malekian, and Ozdaglar show that externalities across
  correlated users depress data prices and distort sharing decisions. Their
  externality runs through correlation among users, while the leakage here
  runs through a provider's learning, but both mechanisms explain why observed
  data prices can understate the value at stake.

- Madsen, Erik, and Nikhil Vellodi. 2025. “Insider Imitation.” *Journal of
  Political Economy* 133(2): 652–709.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.1086/732888).

  Madsen and Vellodi analyze a platform that uses information disclosed by
  third-party sellers to compete with them and study how restricting that use
  affects innovation. Theirs is the closest platform-market counterpart of the
  hidden-reuse hazard studied here.

## Dynamic incentives and bargaining

- Freixas, Xavier, Roger Guesnerie, and Jean Tirole. 1985. “Planning under
  Incomplete Information and the Ratchet Effect.” *Review of Economic Studies*
  52(2): 173–191.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.2307/2297615).

  The ratchet-effect literature motivates the idea that information revealed
  today changes tomorrow's incentives and bargaining environment.

- Nash, John F. 1950. “The Bargaining Problem.” *Econometrica* 18(2): 155–162.
  [DOI](https://doi.org/10.2307/1907266).

  The model uses generalized Nash bargaining to allocate gains over outside
  options in both periods.

## Prior essays in this project

- Soren Larson. 2025. [“The Bitter Lesson and Hayek's Revenge”](https://hypersoren.xyz/posts/hayeks-revenge/).

- Soren Larson. 2025. [“Cybernetic Arbitrage”](https://hypersoren.xyz/posts/cybernetic-arbitrage/).

- Soren Larson. 2026. [“A Complexity Theory of AI Value Accrual”](https://hypersoren.xyz/posts/price-elasticity/).

The first essay explains why physical and economic constraints keep relevant
context local. The second argues that ownership of context-generating assets
can become an equilibrium response. The third separates provider pricing power
from participation in the operating value its models create. The models in this
package supply contracting, scale, network-access, and appropriation mechanisms
connecting those claims.
